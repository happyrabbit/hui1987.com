22.2 Living History Farms

Foodways: The Ioway made use of Iowa’s prairies, rivers and wooded valleys to find food. They hunted and fished for meat. They gathered wild plants. The Ioway farmed along the rivers. Many foods were eaten fresh, while others were dried and stored for the rest of the year. The Ioway ate fresh food in season. They ate some foods raw, but also cooked many foods at ta fire pit. Each family prepared their own daily meals. Iowa women preserved food by drying it. They cut fresh meat into thin slices and hung it to dry in the sun. Ears of corn were hung from poles or speed on platforms to dry on the vine and then shell them. Squash were sliced into thin rings and hung on frames to dry. The Ioway used dried foods to make stews, the main dish in their diet. Dried meat was also pulverized and mixed with berries and animal fat to make pemmican. Dried corn could be ground into flour for breads and thickeners.

Storing Food: The Ioway stored dried foods in cache pits, bell-shaped holes dug into the ground. The cache pit could be up to five feet deep. Women first lined the pit with bark or grass to keep put moisture and pests. The Ioway then added ears of corn along the outside, filling the center with ben and then squash. The opening was covered by layers of animal hide, grass, planks and ashes.
